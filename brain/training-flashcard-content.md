# Training Flashcard Content (video-specific, replaces old generic deck)

Source: Brayden's transcripts of the 8 locked videos (see [[training-videos]]). All content below is original paraphrase, not verbatim transcript text. 6 cards per video × 8 videos = 48 cards, same total count as the old deck it replaces.

## Category 1 — AI RECEPTIONIST (Video 1)

1. Q: How do you put a dollar value on one missed call a day?
   A: Multiply missed calls/day × average ticket price × days per year — e.g. 1 missed call/day at $300/ticket ≈ $100K/year in lost revenue.
2. Q: What's the simplest way to describe what an AI receptionist is?
   A: A highly conversational AI voice agent that sounds human enough most callers can't tell it's not a person.
3. Q: What's the difference between a templated AI receptionist and a custom one?
   A: Templated (e.g. RingCentral, GoHighLevel) is plug-and-play but generic; custom (built on platforms like Retell AI or Vapi) is tailored to the business but needs technical expertise, usually via an agency.
4. Q: Does an AI receptionist have to replace human staff?
   A: No — a business owner can choose full AI coverage 24/7, or AI only as overflow when humans can't get to the phone (busy hours, after hours).
5. Q: Why is voicemail considered a lost cause compared to an AI receptionist?
   A: Most callers won't leave a voicemail — they just call the next business that actually answers.
6. Q: Roughly what does usage-based AI receptionist pricing look like?
   A: Raw cost is around $0.12/minute; agencies typically charge a flat monthly rate (e.g. ~$200/mo for a set number of minutes) plus an overage rate per minute beyond that.

## Category 2 — TONALITY (Video 2)

1. Q: What three things do you need to establish in the first four seconds of a call?
   A: That you're sharp, enthusiastic, and an expert in your field.
2. Q: Roughly how much of communication impact comes from tonality and body language versus the actual words?
   A: About 90% combined (tonality + body language), only about 10% from the words themselves.
3. Q: Is charisma something you're born with?
   A: No — it's a learnable skill built from effective tonality, appropriate body language, and not saying things that undercut you.
4. Q: What two things does a prospect need to feel for rapport to form?
   A: That you genuinely care about them, and that you're "just like them" — a sense of commonality/connection.
5. Q: Why does using deliberate tonality give you more control of a conversation?
   A: It forces the listener to unconsciously work to figure out what you really mean, pulling them into engaging with you instead of tuning out.
6. Q: What's the risk of having no tonality at all when you speak?
   A: People mentally check out — without vocal emphasis there's nothing pulling their attention into what you're saying.

## Category 3 — DISCOVERY (Video 3)

1. Q: What's the purpose of a clarifying/probing question like "how do you mean by that?"
   A: It gets the prospect to go deeper than a surface answer and reveal what's really driving the problem.
2. Q: Why repeat a prospect's own emotional word back to them (e.g. "stressed")?
   A: It invites them to elaborate on that feeling instead of staying vague, opening them up.
3. Q: What does asking "how long has that been going on for?" accomplish?
   A: It gets the prospect to relive how long the problem has been affecting them, deepening the felt pain.
4. Q: Why pause after asking something like "...has that had an impact on you"?
   A: The pause forces a deeper, more considered answer instead of a reflexive one.
5. Q: Name an open-ended discovery question that works across any industry.
   A: Examples: "What's causing this to happen?" / "What's prompting you to look into changing this now?" / "What would it mean for you to solve this problem?"
6. Q: Why ask discovery questions before pitching at all?
   A: Surfacing the prospect's real pain and emotions first builds urgency — pitching cold gets ignored, pitching after pain is surfaced gets attention.

## Category 4 — GATEKEEPER (Video 4)

1. Q: What's the actual goal when a gatekeeper answers the phone?
   A: Get past them to the decision-maker (owner or exec) — only someone who can act on your offer is worth pitching.
2. Q: Why avoid a formal self-introduction like "Hi, this is [name] calling from [company]"?
   A: It signals "sales call" instantly and gives the gatekeeper an easy reason to screen you out.
3. Q: What tone should you use when asking for the decision-maker?
   A: Casual and familiar — as if you already have a relationship with them, not overly professional or scripted.
4. Q: What should you do immediately after asking your question to the gatekeeper?
   A: Pause and let them respond — talking more to fill the silence undercuts your control of the call.
5. Q: Why does over-explaining yourself to a gatekeeper hurt you?
   A: It reads as nervous or salesy, the opposite of sounding like someone who belongs on that call.
6. Q: What's the underlying principle behind sounding "casual" instead of "professional" on a cold open?
   A: People are more guarded around anything that looks polished/sales-like — casual reads as safe and familiar.

## Category 5 — OBJECTIONS (Video 5)

1. Q: Why do prospects give objections like "I need to think about it" or "send me more info"?
   A: They're stuck in a frame where they feel no real urgency to change.
2. Q: What are the two emotional drivers that cause someone to actually change?
   A: Pain (current) and fear of future pain.
3. Q: What's a "consequence question" used for?
   A: To walk the prospect through what happens if they do nothing — chaining "and then what happens?" deeper until it hits something personal (family, career, identity).
4. Q: What's an "identity frame" reframe?
   A: Contrasting the prospect against people who never fixed the problem and got stuck, so they don't want to identify with that outcome.
5. Q: Why use a concerned tone when asking a tough question like "how can I tell you this without you getting upset?"
   A: It seeds doubt — the prospect senses you know something they don't, which lowers their guard.
6. Q: What's the underlying mistake of rebuttal-style objection handling?
   A: It argues with the prospect's existing frame instead of reframing how they see the problem altogether.

## Category 6 — QUALIFYING (Video 6)

1. Q: What's the basic household-income idea for qualifying someone financially?
   A: Their income + spouse's income should comfortably exceed what's needed to afford the payment, since the product is meant to pay for itself over time.
2. Q: Why doesn't existing debt automatically disqualify a prospect?
   A: If their income comfortably covers the payment and the product itself improves their results/income, the payment isn't a real cash-flow problem.
3. Q: What approach works well when qualifying someone around money?
   A: Ask like a coach — about their history and relationship with money/success — rather than just demanding numbers.
4. Q: What should you do if a prospect clearly can't afford the offer?
   A: Qualify them out quickly and move to the next lead rather than spending time trying to make it work.
5. Q: What's the benefit of moving fast through unqualified leads?
   A: It keeps your overall call efficiency high — a few extra minutes either way doesn't change a clearly unqualified outcome.
6. Q: Why ask about someone's past relationship with money/success before pitching price?
   A: It surfaces whether they see themselves as someone who invests in solving problems, which shapes how you frame the offer.

## Category 7 — BOOKING (Video 7)

1. Q: Why have the prospect's info pulled up before calling them back?
   A: Re-asking questions they already answered on a form makes them feel like you're wasting their time.
2. Q: What's the disconnect between how setters and prospects view pushiness?
   A: Far more prospects perceive salespeople as pushy than salespeople think they are — there's a real gap in self-awareness here.
3. Q: When is it actually okay to get a little more assertive in a call?
   A: After you've built rapport, shown value, and qualified the prospect — not before.
4. Q: Why ask "do you have two minutes?" instead of not asking at all?
   A: It's a small, easy yes that respects their time, especially with warmer prospects expecting your call.
5. Q: Why assume the appointment instead of asking "would you like to move forward?"
   A: Asking gives them an easy opening to say no, even if they were close to yes.
6. Q: When is the best moment to ask for a referral?
   A: When the prospect is expressing gratitude — that's when they're most open to recommending you.

## Category 8 — TIME MANAGEMENT (Video 8)

1. Q: What's the most common reason reps fail to hit their numbers, per this training?
   A: Not laziness — a lack of structure, reacting to whatever comes up instead of planning the day.
2. Q: Why are morning dial blocks considered the most valuable call time?
   A: Prospects haven't filled up their day with meetings yet, so connect rates are higher.
3. Q: Why dedicate a specific block to sourcing instead of doing it whenever?
   A: It's easy to skip when unstructured, but it's what builds tomorrow's pipeline.
4. Q: What's the value of a few minutes of passive learning (e.g. listening to top rep calls) over lunch?
   A: Low-effort exposure to what's working, without taking deep focus away from active calling/selling time.
5. Q: Why end the day with a short reflection on what worked?
   A: Without reviewing, activity doesn't turn into improvement — you repeat the same mistakes.
6. Q: What's the core mindset shift behind a structured day?
   A: You don't need to work longer — you need to work intentionally, protecting call blocks like they're meetings.
