# Ohvara — Setter Discovery Script (Question-Based Diagnostic Model)

*Canonical, universal (niche-agnostic) setter call script. Last set:
2026-06-15. This is the ONE setter script model — niche-agnostic by design
(`[niche]` / `[Business Name]` tokens), consistent with the "ONE universal
Training Center rewrite, not per-niche" decision in
[[ohvara-pricing-packaging-strategy]]. Maps onto the dashboard's existing
color-coded script sections (Opener / Discovery / Pain Amplification /
Handoff / Objections).*

**Core model:** the setter is the **intake nurse** — gathering the full
picture of the prospect's pain so **Nate (closer)** walks into the call
already knowing the diagnosis and the right plan. The setter **never pitches,
prices, or explains the product.** Every question should make the prospect
more aware of their own problem, not tell them about it. This is the
two-axis model in action: **niche = setter axis, problem = closer axis** —
the setter qualifies the problem, Nate builds the fix.

> **Warm not cold (training framing):** these are not cold leads. Each prospect
> **self-identified** the missed-call / understaffing problem by posting the
> Indeed job. The setter is following up on a problem the owner already
> admitted — see [[ohvara-pricing-packaging-strategy]].

---

## 1. Opener
*Goal: survive the first 10 seconds. Sound like a peer who noticed something specific, not a salesperson.*

> **Tone first — this is the whole game.** Every real call recording from AI agency sellers says the same thing: it's not the words, it's how you sound. Be casual. Almost laid-back. Sound like a friend calling a friend, not a rep working a list. Drop your energy a half-notch from "professional" — warmer, unhurried. If you're monotone or stiff, no script saves you. Practice out loud until this sounds like you, not like a script.

### Standard Opener (Indeed lead — use this 90% of the time)

> **Why this works:** you have a real, specific reason to call — they posted the job. That's better than any fake pattern interrupt. Don't waste it. Lead with it before you say who you are.

1. Confirm you have the right person first:
   *"Hey, is this [Business Name]? Is [First Name] around?"* — pause, let them say yes.

2. Lead with context (the Indeed post = your reason):
   *"Hey [First Name] — saw you guys have a listing up for a [receptionist/dispatcher/front desk]. Quick question about how you're handling calls while that search is going — you got a minute?"*

3. **Stop there. Do not explain anything else.** Let them respond.

> **What not to say:** don't say "I'm calling from [company]" yet. Don't say "we help businesses like yours." Don't use "how are you doing" — it flags you as a telemarketer. The job posting is already your credibility. Use it.

---

### Pattern Interrupt Opener (no Indeed listing, or lead went cold)

> Use when you don't have an active Indeed post but you're calling into a warm niche. The "Google says closed" angle from real AI receptionist cold calls — gets two easy yeses before you say anything of substance.

*"Hey, sorry — did you just say [Business Name]? You guys still open? It said closed on Google for some reason."*
— [they confirm they're open]
*"Oh weird, might be a glitch — anyway, quick question while I have you…"*
— [transition to the vitals check opener question below]

> Casual, almost unprofessional. You're not trying to sound polished. You're trying to sound like a customer who accidentally called.

---

### Permission Frame (use when they seem hesitant after the first line)

> If they don't immediately engage, give them an explicit out. This sounds counterintuitive but it lowers their guard more than any other line — because it signals you're not desperate.

*"Look, I know this is out of nowhere — you can totally tell me you're slammed and I'll let you go. Quick question first though: while you're looking for that person, who's catching the phones when your team's tied up?"*

---

### Bridge into Discovery

Whichever opener you used, this is the transition line once they're talking:
*"Quick question — while that search is going, what's actually happening right now when a call comes in and nobody's free to grab it?"*

This is the first discovery question. It's also the last thing you say in the opener — after this you're in Section 2 (Vitals Check).

> **Coach's note:** the opener's only job is to earn the next 30 seconds. The moment they answer any question, you've earned it — move to Vitals. Don't fill silence with explanation. Don't preview what you do. The second you say "we have an AI that..." you've lost the intake-nurse frame and they'll either shut down or ask about price before you've built any value.

## 2. The Vitals Check (Discovery)
*Goal: get the real numbers — call volume, leakage, cost, and why now. Every answer feeds Nate's pre-call brief and the AI's recommendation. Dig for specifics, not "yeah it's busy."*

> **How to ask questions in this section:** always give them the reason you're asking before you ask it — "I only ask because..." or "the reason I'm wondering is..." This is what separates a diagnostic conversation from an interrogation. When people know why you want a number, they give you a real one instead of a deflection.

> **Point of view opener (use to bridge from the opener):** Before launching in, drop one short credibility statement to get them thinking — it prompts them to open up without you having to extract it. *"Most [niche] owners I talk to, their crew is out on jobs and calls are the thing that slips through the cracks the most — even after they've tried to fix it. Is that kind of the situation, or is it something different for you?"* — then shut up and let them answer. Whatever they say is the thread you pull.

---

**Current setup**
- *"Walk me through what happens right now when a call comes in — who picks it up?"*
- *"With [X] techs out in the field, who's actually catching the phone when they're on a job?"*
- *"Is that the same person handling scheduling too, or is it split?"* — I only ask because if one person's doing both, that's usually where the gap shows up.

**Volume & leakage**
- *"On a normal day, how many calls are you getting in?"*
- *"And on your busiest days — the days you're hiring this person for — what does that look like?"*
- *"When nobody gets to it, where does the call go — voicemail, a cell? Does it ever just not get picked up at all?"*
- *"How often would you say that happens — like in a given week?"*

**Cost**
- *"Do you have a rough sense of what one of those calls is worth if it turns into a job?"*
- *"So if a handful of those are slipping through every week — what's that running you a month?"* — let them say the number out loud. Don't do the math for them.
- *"And what's kept you from solving it before now — just the timing, or is it harder than it looks to find the right person?"* — this surfaces whether it's a new problem or a chronic one. If they've tried and failed, urgency is real.

**Why now**
- *"What made now the time to post for this — was there a specific moment, or has it just been building?"*
- *"Is this more of a seasonal crunch, or is it year-round?"*

**Current tools + scope**
- *"Are you using anything right now for after-hours — an answering service, voicemail-to-text, anything? Or does it just ring out?"*
- *"Is it just the one location, or do you have multiple?"*

> **Coach's note:** this should feel like a checkup, not an interrogation. React to what they say — *"oh wow," "that's more than I'd expect," "got it"* — and let silence sit after the cost question. Most owners haven't actually done the math before. Give them a second. The number they say out loud is what Nate will use to anchor the close.

## 3. Pain Amplification
*Goal: build the gap. On one side — what they just told you (calls missed, jobs lost). On the other — every call answered, every job captured. You don't tell them the gap is big. You help them see it, and then you let them say the number.*

> **Read their temperature first.** After the Vitals Check you'll know which category they're in:
> - **Hot** (they confirmed real leakage and said a number) → don't keep asking. Reflect it back and move to the gap question. You've already won this section.
> - **Neutral** (things are "okay," vague, not sure) → name the common pain before asking: *"Most [niche] owners I talk to, even when they feel on top of it, are losing 3-5 jobs a week they never even know about. Does that resonate at all, or do you feel like you've got it covered?"*
> - **Cold** (everything's fine, no problem) → don't push. Move on to the Handoff — a cold lead here probably isn't worth a Nate call anyway.

---

**Reflect the consequence — not just the problem**

Don't say "so you're missing calls." Say what happens *because* of the missed call:
*"So just to make sure I've got this right — when it goes to voicemail, that's not just a missed call. That's probably a job that goes to whoever picks up next. Is that kind of what you're seeing?"*

The consequence is what stings, not the symptom. Name it in their words, stay neutral, and let them confirm.

**Build the gap**

*"So on one side — [their number] calls a week going unanswered, [their estimate] per job. On the other side, every one of those gets picked up, every estimate gets booked. What does that gap look like over a month?"*

Let them do the math out loud. Don't give them the number. The number they say carries 10x the weight of a number you say.

**The year-out question**

*"If nothing changed — same volume, same leakage — what does that add up to by this time next year?"*

This moves them from "it's a problem" to "it's an expensive, ongoing problem." Most owners haven't done this math before. Silence after this question is fine — let it land.

**Flip to results (if they're engaging well)**

*"And if that gap closed — every call answered, no jobs slipping — what would that actually change for [Business Name] day to day?"*

This isn't about the product. It's about their outcome. Get them describing the future they want. Whatever they say, Nate will use it.

> **Coach's note:** stay neutral throughout this section. Don't say "you're *losing* $X" — say "that's potentially $X that's just not getting captured." Assumptive language makes owners get defensive and downplay. Neutral language makes them nod and confirm. If they downplay it, don't argue — just move to the next question or go straight to the Handoff.

## 4. The Handoff (Booking with Nate)
*Goal: transition from pain to appointment without pitching — frame Nate as the specialist who reviews this specific situation and builds the fix, not a closer who delivers a sales pitch.*

> **Where setters lose the meeting:** rushing the ask before the pain has landed, or asking with a weak "can I get 15 minutes?" that sounds identical to every other sales call. Every booking that sticks follows the same pattern: the setter frames Nate's call as a prepared consultation with a specific, concrete outcome for the prospect — not a product demo, not a pitch, not "we'll just chat."

---

### The bridge

Close the pain section and pivot — don't leave a gap. As soon as the prospect has said the number or confirmed the gap, transition immediately:

*"Okay — here's what I want to do. Everything you just told me — the volume, what's slipping, your typical job value — I'm going to pass all of that to Nate, who works specifically with [niche] businesses on this exact problem."*

Don't wait for them to respond. Keep going:

*"He's going to review your situation before the call and put together what would actually make sense for [Business Name] — not a one-size package. It's 15 minutes. He's done this across a lot of [niche] businesses so he'll know pretty quickly if there's something worth doing here."*

Stop there. The ask comes next — don't fill the silence with more explanation.

> **What not to say:** don't say "free consultation," "no obligation," or "just a quick chat." Those signal sales call and trigger resistance. Frame it as Nate reviewing their specific numbers and building something — not presenting something he already built.

---

### The time ask

Always two specific options, never open-ended. Open-ended ("when are you free?") puts work on them and invites vague responses. Two options force a decision:

*"Does [Tuesday morning] or [Wednesday afternoon] work better for you?"*

Once they pick a day, get specific:

*"What time works on [day] — morning or afternoon?"* → then lock it: *"[Time] works. I'll send you a quick text to confirm so you've got it — best number for that?"*

> **After they give a time:** confirm it in one line and move to number/text confirmation immediately. Don't linger. The longer you stay on the line after the booking, the more chances for them to talk themselves out of it.

---

### Soft no recovery (they don't say yes immediately)

A soft no sounds like: *"I'm not sure this week is the right time,"* or *"let me think about it,"* or hesitation without hanging up. This is different from a hard no — the conversation is still open.

**Step 1 — acknowledge (three words):**
*"I'm with you."*

Don't argue, don't reassure. Just acknowledge it.

**Step 2 — re-engage with one question:**
*"Can I ask — is it more the timing, or is it that you're not sure it's worth 15 minutes?"*

Let them answer. Their answer tells you which direction to go.

**Step 3 — reframe the call itself:**
*"I get it — you've probably had people ask for 20 minutes and stay for an hour pitching something that had nothing to do with what you told them. I'm not going to do that. Nate's going to review exactly what you shared with me — if it doesn't fit, he'll tell you in the first five minutes."*

**Step 4 — drop the ask:**
First ask was 15 minutes? Make the second ask shorter:
*"Even just 10 minutes — enough for him to look at your numbers and tell you if there's something real here. Does [day] work?"*

> **Hard no vs. soft no:** if they shut it down clearly ("I'm not interested," hang-up energy, one-word answers), don't push. Get a callback date: *"No problem — when would be a better time to circle back?"* Log it and move on. Two asks max. Pushing past that poisons the lead for Nate.

---

### After the booking

Confirm and close the loop in one breath:

*"Perfect — [day] at [time]. I'll text you right now to confirm. Nate will review everything before the call so you don't have to recap — he'll already have your numbers."*

Send the text immediately while they're still on the line. This closes the loop and dramatically reduces no-shows.

> **Coach's note:** don't preview pricing, features, or mention "the AI" by name. Don't say "we have a product that..." or "what we do is..." That entire conversation belongs to Nate — with the full picture in hand, he builds the frame around their specific pain. If you preview it here you give them something to object to before Nate has a chance to anchor the value. Your job ends the moment the time slot is locked and the confirmation text is sent.

## 5. Booking Objections
*Goal: these are objections to scheduling the call, not objections to having a problem — that part's already settled.*

- **"Just send me some info"** → "Totally get it, but honestly the stuff that matters is specific to what you just told me — Nate can build something around your actual numbers in 15 minutes. Easier to just grab the time."
- **"I don't have time this week"** → "No problem, that's exactly why he keeps it to 15 minutes — what's better, [option A] or [option B]?"
- **"Who's Nate / what company is this?"** → "Fair question — we work with [niche] businesses on exactly the call-coverage issue we just walked through. Nate can explain the whole thing on the call."
- **"How much does this cost?"** → "Honestly depends on your call volume and setup, which is exactly what Nate's going to map out for you — that's why I didn't want to guess at a number before he's seen your numbers."
